01. Project Overview

Abstract

I analyzed how this spy camera detector application offers vague means to understand women’s safety from spy cameras in Korea, and additionally how it wields the potential to abuse women’s fear and learned helplessness. Since the minority group’s social psychology center around these feelings, it should be acknowledged as the design elements when aiming for social impact. Furthermore, this acknowledgement should be followed by continuous trust work between the application operators and the users, or the citizens.

Problem

Can Technology and Design help solve social problems when the targeted user group is emotionally unavailable to use it?

Goal

Although social trust cannot be directly integrated or designed into a system, design could create a better world by designing an environment that has the capacity to encourage the emergence of trust.

Reference

  • This study was primarily based on CHRISTOPHER A. LE DANTEC’s ethnographic approach on social trust and social innovation design. The concepts and research method shown in his articles Designing Civic Technology with Trust, ‘Removing Barriers’ and ‘Creating Distance’: Exploring the Logics of Efficiency and Trust in Civic Technology and Exploring Trust in Digital Civics became a foundation for my research.
  • NASSIM PARVIN - Unintended by Design: On the Political Uses of “Unintended Consequences” and Uber in Bangladesh: The Tangled Web of Mobility and Justice
  • CHRISTINA N. HARRINGTON - Deconstructing Community-Based Collaborative Design: Towards More Equitable Participatory Design Engagements

03. Theoretical Framework
01. Korean Women’s Three Social Psychologies

When the human’s basic desire to autonomously govern their world is largely betrayed by a discriminative social environment, negative psychology is produced. Therefore, this negative psychology is easily associated with minority communities. This demographic is much more susceptible to negative feelings like frustration, helplessness, fear, and depression-- stemming from intangible stigmatization and physical threats.

Fear

  • Daily feeling of being in danger in regards to the epidemic of spy cameras
  • Constant and negative emotion
  • Existential fear of social discrimination as an oppressed group member

Learned Helplessness

  • Feeling stemming from the notion that society cannot be changed with individual’s effort
  • Inconstant and negative emotion
  • Results from the feeling of being unable to achieve social justice and low self-efficacy

Indignation

  • Feelings of resentment against sexism and gender discrimination, often resulting in forming social movement and protest
  • Inconstant and positive emotion
  • Results from the desire to eradicate social injustice, stemming from the belief in relatively higher self-efficacy
But... How?
02. Social Trust engendered by design

When technology is not built with consideration of the minority group, this exclusion will engender the reluctance to adopt the technology, particularly for minority group members, as people can sense that the technology is not intended for them to utilize. On the other hand, appropriate relational association between the application administrators and the users (who are most likely to be Korean women) would engender social trust, which would encourage these people to become actual users.

Trust in design is how one forms expectations, which makes one "leap" over fear and into uncertainty.

Trust Work

: close the distance between the technology [ the application administrators ] — the user group.

: close the distance between Relief’s Map’s administrators [ who are predominently men ] — Korean Women.

Relief’s Map should implement Trust Work within Design,
considering Pervasive Distrust within Korean women of society and men in general.

05. Product Analysis

Intended Process of Relief’s Map

Current Five Main Features

The Relief’s Map consists of five main features. Although its key features are detecting spy cameras and reporting the detected spot through the map, it has other features such as displaying current news articles, a home feed, and a profile resembling a social media account profile. However, these diverse features confused the users about the app’s overall purpose.
Since Relief's Map aims to help victims of spy cameras, it is inevitably linked to social innovation design, which heavily concerns the targeted minority group’s collective social psychology. If learned helplessness is a pervasive feeling within the group, it becomes more difficult for the technology to access the users. Therefore, Relief's Map's social innovation design should be based on trust work and collective social psychology. Consequently, it is pivotal to consider the histories that led to these present dynamics if we are to create change for the future.

06. Redesign

DESIGN.

From the user’s perspective and with an ethnographic approach, I conducted the source of the users' distrust on the Relief's Map operators. Their distrust is heavily embedded in the social environment due to a spy cam porn epidemic. I used the insights from my research to base an initial high level, framing of the system.

Based on the Ethnographic Research,

STEP 1. Find the socio-Cultural sources of distrust in the design

STEP 2. Identify The Socio-Technical Design Components Of That Distrust

STEP 3. Imagine the Alternative social innovation design

The findings from the first two steps were developed and reflected in the design process. The third step, social innovation design was segmented into four generative design strategies as suggested by Le Dantec:

  • 4 Generative Design Strategies :
  • STRATEGY 1. historicize engagement
  • STRATEGY 2. focus on experience
  • STRATEGY 3. mediate expectations
  • STRATEGY 4. preserve relationships

09. Reflection
Discussion & Limitations
Lack of discussion on Definition of women

Unlike many western nations, South Korea is still a homogeneous country where race or ethnicity is not considered a major issue. The transgender community was also not thoroughly considered. Given that transgender community also severly suffers from the spy camera issues, I have categorized those who transitioned from male to female as “women,” but there exist nuances within this construct of gender that have not been fully captured in this paper.


cf) Mapping safe toilets for transgender Americans (link)

Research Location Limited to Seoul

The location of these interview participants were limited to Seoul and the application redesign was done with a focus on the same region.

Incorporation of Participatory Design

: Design technology not only “for” but also “with” the minority communities

This paper in particular includes elements of participatory design, but mainly focuses on ethnography. Participatory Design is envisioned as a design approach to democratizing the design process by shifting the power relationships between researcher and participant. Indeed, there are some limitations, such as the historical context of the research environment, community access, perceptions of materials and activities, and unintentional harm in collecting full accounts of personal narratives. Nevertheless, within the design process, the importance of Participatory Design as a means to innovate society and contribute to social equality is becoming a significant part of the social innovation design and civic design areas. Moreover, it is one of the most effective design methods for the historically oppressed and marginalized. This goes beyond building the trust between the researcher or application administrator and the users who participate in the design process. Instead, the method of participatory design aims to break the boundary between them and therefore seeks to build equal access to the design. In future research, participatory design can be utilized to further inform and expand the set of possible design choices, hoping for designing technology not only “for” but also “with” the minority communities.

Not fully concerning the stake of the For-Profit Parent Corporation

Although this case study is focused on user experiences directly from the minority group members, for the better social innovation design, hearing out the difficulties from both sides – the for-profit corporation and the (potential) users from the devalued social group would have elicited better design results. While the civic design from the government or not-for-profit organization does not consider financial issues as its key consideration for its end goal is only aiming to better society, the for-profit organization creating social innovation design has multiple end goals: to make money as well as to better the community. Although I contacted Relief’s Map’s parent company in December 2021 and could hear out their situation, including their hardships, I was not able to include data from the corporations, which might have been a way to inform a more realistic design. Furthering this case study considering the corporation’s viewpoint would render other insights to the HCI and User Experience design academia and communities.

Conclusion

Designing technology for minority communities has become a pivotal topic not only within not-for-profit organizations but also for for-profit corporations. Given our current social context, attention towards the emergence and prevalence of for-profits with the primary concerns in minority communities’ well-being is long overdue. Nonetheless, in order for these organizations to contribute to social justice, it is crucial to first comprehend the complex reality of and social psychologies within minority groups.
Through this case study, I insist on how design wields the potential to abuse women’s fear and learned helplessness, even unintentionally. Since the end goal of the product design should be based on eliciting the optimal user experience, I suggest the usefulness of including the authentic voices of (potential) users. This is as an effort to not misrepresent the user needs as well as the company’s genuine intentions to help these marginalized groups of people. With a better understanding of the complexity of the minority group’s social psychology mentioned in this paper, the design and technology suggestions could pave a way for understanding technology’s place in social justice and transform the ways we protect our women and communities. ✧
